The **International Designator**, also known as COSPAR ID, and NSSDC ID, is an international identifier assigned to man‚Äëmade objects in space. It consists of the launch year, a three‚Äëdigit incrementing launch number of that year and up to a three‚Äëletter code representing the sequential identifier of a piece in a launch. In TLE format the first two digits of the year and the dash are dropped.

For example, **1990-037A** is the Space Shuttle Discovery on mission STS-31, which carried the Hubble Space Telescope (**1990-037B**) into space. This launch was the 37th known successful launch worldwide in 1990.

–ê–ª—å—Ç–µ—Ä–Ω–∞—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–æ–π —Å–∏—Å—Ç–µ–º–æ–π –æ–±–æ–∑–Ω–∞—á–µ–Ω–∏—è –∏—Å–∫—É—Å—Å—Ç–≤–µ–Ω–Ω—ã—Ö —Å–ø—É—Ç–Ω–∏–∫–æ–≤ —è–≤–ª—è–µ—Ç—Å—è **–Ω–æ–º–µ—Ä –ø–æ —Å–ø—É—Ç–Ω–∏–∫–æ–≤–æ–º—É –∫–∞—Ç–∞–ª–æ–≥—É (SCN)**.

The designation system has been generally known as the COSPAR system, named for the [Committee on Space Research (COSPAR)](cospar.md) of the International Council for Science. COSPAR subsumed the first designation system, devised at Harvard University in 1958. That system used letters of the Greek alphabet to designate artificial satellites. For example, Sputnik‚ÄØ1 was designated 1957 Alpha‚ÄØ2. The Harvard designation system continued to be used for satellites launched up to the end of 1962, when it was replaced with the modern system. The first satellite to receive a new‚Äëformat designator was Luna E-6 No.2, 1963-001B, although some sources, including the NSSDC website, anachronistically apply the new‚Äëformat designators to older satellites, even those no longer in orbit at the time of its introduction.

Designators are assigned to objects by USSTRATCOM along with satellite catalog numbers as they are discovered in space. The United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs (UNOOSA) and the National Space Science Data Center (NSSDC), part of NASA, maintain two catalogs that provide additional information on the launchers and payloads associated with the designators. Spacecraft which do not complete an orbit of the Earth, for example launches which fail to achieve orbit, are not assigned IDs.

Objects that fail to orbit or orbit for a short time are not catalogued. The minimum object size in the catalog is 10‚ÄØcentimeters in diameter. As of June‚ÄØ23, 2019, the catalog listed 44‚ÄØ336‚ÄØobjects including 8‚ÄØ558‚ÄØsatellites launched into orbit since 1957. 17‚ÄØ480 of them were actively tracked while 1‚ÄØ335 were lost. [ESA](zz_esa.md) estimates there are about 34‚ÄØ000 orbiting debris of the size USSTRATCOM is capable to track as of January‚ÄØ2019.

USSTRATCOM shares the catalog via <http://space-track.org> website. 18th Space Control Squadron (18 SPCS) is the unit that maintains the catalog.
